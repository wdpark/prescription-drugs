The Solution:


Ultimately, the U.S. government is the only entity that can exercise control over the pharmaceutical drug industry and possibly reach a compromise. The problem of high prices does not have a simple solution, however, since there isn’t just one problem: there’s multiple.


Problem: Some drug manufacturers take advantage of the natural monopoly of some drugs to artificially increase the prices.

Possible Solution: The U.S. Food and Drug Administration can implement incentive programs to encourage competition such as voucher programs for entering a sole-source market or expedited-review programs. The U.S. government can also revise the Hatch-Waxman Act that delays the introduction of generic drugs into the market by 30 months and 180 day exclusivity for first generic drugs. These actions will ultimately increase competition to the point where lower prices will garner more profit.


Problem: The Pharmaceutical Drug industry does not provide pricing information available at the point of care, making it difficult for patients to make the best decisions.


Solution: The U.S. government can require a certain degree of transparency from drug companies regarding prices such as a report on rebates and prices paid. Investing in comparative-effectiveness research would also help confirm the overall efficacy of certain treatments.

Problem: Federal law imposes restrictions on state authority to lower prices for Medicaid and reduce drug prices.

Possible Solution: Allow states to manage pharmacy benefit management (PBMs) to expand their authority and purchasing power in their negotiations.
